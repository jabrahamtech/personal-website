Why production voice AI fails after the demo

category voice_ai_in_production read ~ 7 min posted 2026-04

Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet, consectetur adipiscing elit. Every voice AI demo is impressive. The model speaks fluently, handles a couple of friendly turns, and the founder claims they've replaced a phone team. Two weeks into production, the same system is silently dropping calls, repeating itself, and confidently inventing customer details.

The hard part is not the voice. The hard part is everything around the voice.

The four things demos hide

1. State across turns

Sed do eiusmod tempor incididunt ut labore et dolore magna aliqua. A demo is two or three turns. Production is twenty. State is not "what's in the context window" — it's the operational record that survives a hangup, a redial, and a handoff.

2. Interruptions

Real callers interrupt. They cough. They turn to talk to someone else. They hang up mid-sentence. Every node in your flow has to be cancellable — not "throw an exception", cancellable. Most demos can't even pause cleanly.

3. Memory you can audit

Duis aute irure dolor in reprehenderit in voluptate velit esse cillum dolore. The moment you put a voice agent in front of a paying customer, "the model said something" stops being acceptable. You need a structured record: what was asked, what was understood, what was decided, what was acted on.

4. Handoff

The voice agent is rarely the system of record. It's a collector. The handoff to the CRM, the ticketing system, or the human queue has to be idempotent, retryable, and provenance-tagged. Most demos handwave this with a webhook.

If your demo can't survive being interrupted, redialled, and handed off mid-call, it's not a system. It's a video.

The LLM gets called only when it has to be, with a strict schema for what it can return, and a deterministic state machine vetoing anything that violates the call's required fields.

If you're building one

  1. Write the call as a state machine on paper before you touch a model.
  2. Make every node cancellable. Test by hanging up at every step.
  3. Log everything the model said and why. Every field needs a source.
  4. Treat handoff as a separate, retryable system. Not a callback.
  5. Demo with hostile callers, not friendly ones.
